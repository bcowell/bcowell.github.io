    Job Description: My job title at CCS was Junior Developer. I worked as part of a small team of Senior Analysts in Human Resources of the Enterprise Applications section of CCS. I was tasked to develop, re-theme, and service a few of the University's HR related web applications. The University of Guelph has plans to move to a new HR Management system to new servers within the next few years.

    Work: I spent a fair amount of time migrating old applications in Apex into a new theme in preparation of migration to Apex 5.
    Another of my accomplishments was the complete refactoring of one of our Apex pages. The Appointments page was a behemoth of spaghetti code. I found redundant and reused queries, unnecessary computations and many parts were difficult to understand. Of the first things to go; A dynamic procedure that queried all the page items individually and then set them as global (application level) variables, where they would be referenced with page-level substitution variables. Other problems included obscure and unnecessarily lengthy code segments. I wanted to improve on the general readability for future debugging. The old Apex page was trying to do too much all at once. It worked by having many regions that it would restrict based on a passed request, thus making it very difficult to debug. I managed to separate two similar operations (Adding a new appointment and promoting an appointment) and created a new page, reducing the mess. In the end I managed to reduce much of the old clutter without losing any functionality.

    Additionally I wrote many PL/SQL procedures and functions. For example, my team was working on a tool to help expedite an existing payment process for our users. So, given the types of input parameters and the expected output. I wrote the code so that it could be easily integrated into our current system. One of my procedures is used to determine the file-types of BLOBs (a collection of binary data stored as a single object in a database). Each file has a hex signature stored in the beginning of its encoding. So my procedure read through the first 8 or 16 bytes of the raw blob file and recognized the file-type.

    A memorable highlight was when we received C code from an outside software distributor that wouldn't compile on our payroll system, I found the code segments causing the errors and re-wrote them so that it would compile. And by doing so, saved over a week of time before the software distributor responded to emails.

    Introduction:
    The development process of a website at Sandbox went like this:
    1. The clients would meet with a project coordinator, designer, and a few developers where the project scope, timeline, cost and difficulty are discussed.
    2. The designers and developers work together to create a site map and an overview of the website.
    3. The designer goes through stages of creating what the site is going to look like using wireframing, paper-prototypes, and mockups. Once these have client approval they are made into PSDs.
    4. The Photoshop documents are given to the developers, who work their magic and produce the website in a series of development stages. Development (dev) -> Testing (test) -> Production (prod)
    5. After the developers and clients have thoroughly tested and reviews the site it is pushed live!
    As a Web Developer at Sandbox, I was involved in the later, and largest stages of the project. I was given Photoshop documents (PSDs) of the designs and developed, tested and QA'ed a multitude of websites.
